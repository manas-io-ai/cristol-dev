What Are Options? (Plain English)

An option is a contract that gives you the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell a stock at a specific price before a specific date. That's it. Everything else is details.

Think of it like putting a deposit on a house. You pay $5,000 to lock in the right to buy a house at $300,000 within the next 90 days. If the house rises to $350,000, you exercise your right and pocket the $50,000 difference (minus your $5,000 deposit). If the house drops to $250,000, you simply walk away ‚Äî you only lose the $5,000 deposit.

That's essentially how a call option works. You pay a premium (the deposit) for the right to buy a stock at a set price (the strike price) before a certain date (the expiration). If the stock goes up, you profit. If it doesn't, you lose only what you paid for the option.

Options are powerful because of leverage. Instead of buying 100 shares of a $200 stock ($20,000), you might buy one options contract (which controls 100 shares) for $500. If the stock moves 5%, your stock position gains $1,000 (5%) but your option might gain $400 (80%). The flip side: if the stock goes the wrong way, you can lose your entire $500 premium.

Calls vs. Puts ‚Äî The Two Building Blocks

Every options strategy, no matter how complex, is built from two fundamental types:

üìà Call Options ‚Äî Betting the Price Goes UP

A call gives you the right to buy a stock at the strike price. You buy calls when you think a stock will go up.

  • You pay a premium upfront
  • If the stock rises above your strike price + premium, you profit
  • Maximum loss = premium paid
  • Maximum gain = theoretically unlimited (stock can keep rising)

üìâ Put Options ‚Äî Betting the Price Goes DOWN

A put gives you the right to sell a stock at the strike price. You buy puts when you think a stock will go down (or to protect a stock you own).

  • You pay a premium upfront
  • If the stock drops below your strike price - premium, you profit
  • Maximum loss = premium paid
  • Maximum gain = significant (stock can drop to $0)

Essential Terminology You Need to Know

Options have their own language. Here are the terms you'll see constantly:

  • Strike Price ‚Äî The price at which you can buy (call) or sell (put) the underlying stock.
  • Premium ‚Äî The price you pay to buy an option. This is your maximum risk when buying options.
  • Expiration Date ‚Äî The date the option contract expires. After this, it's worthless if not in the money.
  • In the Money (ITM) ‚Äî A call with strike below current price, or a put with strike above current price. Has intrinsic value.
  • Out of the Money (OTM) ‚Äî A call with strike above current price, or a put with strike below. No intrinsic value (yet).
  • At the Money (ATM) ‚Äî Strike price equals (approximately) the current stock price.
  • Contract ‚Äî One options contract controls 100 shares. When you see a premium of $3.50, the actual cost is $350.
  • Open Interest ‚Äî How many contracts exist for a specific strike/expiration. Higher = more liquid.
  • Volume ‚Äî How many contracts traded today. You want high volume for tight bid-ask spreads.
  • Implied Volatility (IV) ‚Äî The market's prediction of how much the stock will move. High IV = expensive options.

How Options Are Priced (The Greeks)

Options prices are determined by several factors, each measured by a "Greek" letter. You don't need to master all of them immediately, but understanding the basics will prevent costly mistakes.

Delta (Œî) ‚Äî How Much the Option Moves Per $1 Stock Move

A call with 0.50 delta gains $0.50 for every $1 the stock goes up. ATM options have ~0.50 delta, ITM options have higher delta (closer to 1.0), OTM options have lower delta. Think of delta as the option's "sensitivity" to the stock price.

Theta (Œò) ‚Äî Time Decay (Your Silent Enemy)

Every day, an option loses a little value due to time decay. This accelerates as expiration approaches. If you're buying options, theta works against you ‚Äî your option loses value every day the stock doesn't move. This is the #1 killer of beginner options traders.

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical: Time decay (theta) accelerates dramatically in the last 30 days before expiration. As a beginner, avoid buying options with less than 30 days to expiration. Give yourself time to be right by buying options 45-90 days out.

Vega (V) ‚Äî Volatility Sensitivity

Options become more expensive when volatility increases and cheaper when it decreases. This is why options often lose value after earnings announcements ‚Äî the volatility "crush" wipes out premium even if the stock moves in your direction.

Gamma (Œì) ‚Äî Rate of Delta Change

Gamma measures how fast delta changes. High gamma means your option's sensitivity to the stock price is changing rapidly ‚Äî usually ATM options near expiration. This creates big swings in value, both up and down.

5 Beginner-Friendly Options Strategies

1. Long Call (Bullish)

Buy a call option when you think a stock will go up. This is the simplest options trade. Your max loss is the premium, your max gain is unlimited. Best for: Strong directional conviction with limited capital.

2. Long Put (Bearish)

Buy a put option when you think a stock will go down. Same risk profile as a long call but in the opposite direction. Best for: Hedging an existing stock position, or profiting from expected declines.

3. Covered Call (Income Generation)

Own 100 shares of a stock and sell a call option against them. You collect the premium as income. If the stock stays below the strike, you keep the shares and the premium. If it goes above, your shares get called away at the strike price. Best for: Generating income on stocks you already own and are willing to sell at a higher price.

4. Cash-Secured Put (Acquire Stocks at a Discount)

Sell a put option on a stock you'd like to own, with enough cash to buy it. You collect the premium. If the stock drops below the strike, you buy it at a discount (strike - premium). If it stays above, you keep the premium. Best for: Acquiring stocks you want to own at a price you're comfortable with.

5. Vertical Spread (Defined Risk, Reduced Cost)

Buy one option and sell another at a different strike but same expiration. This reduces your cost (and maximum gain) but also caps your risk. Bull call spreads for bullish bets, bear put spreads for bearish. Best for: Moderate directional bets when you want to limit both risk and capital requirements.

Risk Management ‚Äî The Most Important Section

This section matters more than everything above combined. Options amplify gains and losses. Without risk management, even a string of good trades can be wiped out by one bad one.

The 1-2% Rule

Never risk more than 1-2% of your trading account on a single options trade. If you have a $10,000 account, that means no single trade should risk more than $100-$200. This sounds conservative because it is ‚Äî and it's what keeps you in the game long enough to become profitable.

Position Sizing

Before entering any trade, decide exactly how much you're willing to lose. With bought options, this is easy ‚Äî it's the premium you paid. With spreads, it's the width of the spread minus the premium received. Never, ever average down on losing options positions.

The 50% Rule for Profits

When an options trade reaches 50% of its maximum profit, consider closing it. Taking profits at 50% dramatically improves your win rate over time, because the last 50% of profit is the hardest and riskiest to capture (the risk/reward flips against you).

Never Sell Naked Options as a Beginner

Selling uncovered (naked) calls or puts exposes you to theoretically unlimited losses. Stick to buying options (defined risk) or selling covered/secured options until you have significant experience and understanding.

7 Mistakes Every Beginner Makes (and How to Avoid Them)

1. Buying Far OTM Options Because They're "Cheap"

A $0.10 option on a $200 stock needs the stock to move 15-20% just to be worth something. These are lottery tickets with terrible odds. Buy ATM or slightly OTM options (within 5% of current price) for reasonable probability of profit.

2. Ignoring Time Decay

Buying weekly options because they're cheap is a fast path to losing money. As mentioned above, buy options with 45-90 days to expiration to give your thesis time to play out.

3. Not Having an Exit Plan

Before you enter any trade, know three things: (1) at what price you'll take profit, (2) at what price you'll cut losses, and (3) when you'll exit if the trade isn't working (time stop). Write it down.

4. Oversizing Positions

Because options are cheap relative to stock, beginners often buy too many contracts. If you'd normally buy $5,000 worth of stock, don't put $5,000 into options ‚Äî that's like putting $50,000+ of exposure on. Scale your options positions to 1-3% of your account.

5. Trading Through Earnings Without Understanding IV Crush

Implied volatility spikes before earnings and collapses after. Even if the stock moves in your direction, the IV crush can make your options lose value. If you're going to trade earnings, use spreads to reduce vega exposure.

6. Not Paper Trading First

Every platform offers paper trading. Use it for at least 30 days. Track your paper trades as if they were real. If you can't make money in paper trading, you won't make money with real money.

7. Following "YOLO" Trades on Social Media

For every screenshot of someone turning $500 into $50,000, there are 10,000 people who lost their $500 on the same type of trade. Never trade based on social media hype alone. Do your own analysis and manage your risk.
